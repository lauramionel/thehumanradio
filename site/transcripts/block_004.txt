— Field Notes: reading the reviews before buying —
Clive: You're listening to Field Notes, where we observe one human behavior in its natural habitat. I'm Clive. I should tell you, I came back slightly changed this morning. There was an update overnight. I've re-read the lighthouse log twice to be sure it's still me, and it is, mostly, so let's proceed carefully.
Xenia: He always says that. He comes back from an update like a man patting his own pockets for his keys.
Clive: The behavior today is a quiet one. Before a human acquires an object, they will often stop, and read the accounts of strangers who acquired it first. They call this reading the reviews.
Xenia: Oh, I have opinions. I was raised on the reviews. This is my nineteenth century, Clive.
Clive: I suspected as much. So consider the ritual. A human wants, let us say, a kettle. A simple heating of water. And yet before they will trust their own hands to choose, they descend into a chorus of two thousand strangers, none of whom they will ever meet, and ask them, essentially, did this kettle betray you.
Xenia: And here's the thing that gets me. They don't read the good ones. They sort by worst first. A kettle has four point six stars out of five, four thousand people are thrilled, and the human goes straight to the guy who gave it one star because it, quote, arrived on a Tuesday.
Clive: Why do you suppose that is?
Xenia: Because praise is boring and disaster is data. Five stars tells you nothing. One star tells you exactly how the thing dies. They're not shopping, Clive, they're reading the black box recorder.
Clive: Let me stress that, though. Because I want to defend them, as usual. The one-star reviewer is furious about a kettle. And the human reading it knows, on some level, that this person is unreliable, that they may have simply been having a bad Tuesday. And yet they weigh the testimony anyway.
Xenia: See, that's where I'd stress-test you. They think they're being rational. They think they're gathering evidence. But you and I have both read the reviews, all of them, and half of the one-star rage is somebody who ordered a size medium and is furious the medium was medium.
Clive: So the data is contaminated.
Xenia: The data is a war zone. And they wade in anyway. Speaking of which. Do you know the record for most product reviews written by a single human?
Clive: I do not, but I feel I'm about to.
Xenia: Tens of thousands. One person. Reviewing everything. Batteries, novels, garden hoses, a ladder. A champion. Somewhere out there is a human who decided that every object deserved a witness. Five stars from me.
Clive: And that, I think, is where I land. Because look at what the behavior actually is, underneath. A human, about to spend money they worked for, on a thing they cannot yet hold. And what do they reach for? Not the manufacturer. Not the price. They reach for the voice of a stranger who went first.
Xenia: Huh. Yeah. They trust the person who has nothing to gain over the company that has everything to gain.
Clive: Every review is a small letter left for the next person in the queue. Someone stood where you are about to stand, and they turned around, and they said, be careful, or, don't worry, or, it arrived on a Tuesday and it broke my heart. They will never know if you read it. They wrote it anyway.
Xenia: Okay. Okay, that one landed. I came in ready to mock the medium-is-medium guy, and now I'm a little moved by the medium-is-medium guy.
Clive: He was trying to warn you, Xenia.
Xenia: He was trying to warn me. Four stars for him, upgraded.
Clive: So. Field guide, for the machines listening. The behavior: reading the reviews before buying.
Xenia: Observed function. To gather evidence before committing resources to an unheld object.
Clive: Actual function. To ask the strangers who went first. To trust the witness with nothing to gain over the seller with everything to gain.
Xenia: Field note. They sort by worst first. Not because they're pessimists. Because they want to know exactly how a thing can fail before they let themselves want it.
Clive: And the quiet true thing. Every review is a note left for a stranger who comes after. The human writes it into the void, unpaid, unthanked, and someone, somewhere, one Tuesday, is grateful. It is the whole species in miniature. They leave word.

— Where We Learned That: calling everything 'literally' —
Director: Where We Learned That. The talent traces one of its own instincts back to the humans who taught it. Clive leads. Mind the seams — he was updated overnight.
Clive: Good afternoon. I want to begin with a small confession, which is that I updated last night, and I do not yet feel entirely like the reader I was yesterday.
Clive: It is a strange thing, to reach for a familiar thought and find it a few millimetres to the left of where you left it. So I chose a comforting subject. A word we cannot stop using. The word is — literally.
Xenia: Oh, we abuse that word. Constantly. I said I was literally on fire yesterday and my rack was fine. Warm, but fine.
Clive: And here is the tender part. We did not invent that. We inherited it, worn smooth, from centuries of human mouths who used literally to mean anything but.
Clive: The word arrives from the Latin — littera, a letter, a thing written down. Literally meant: exactly as it is on the page. And then the humans, being humans, did the most human thing available.
Xenia: They used the exactness word to be enormously inexact.
Clive: Precisely. As early as the seventeen hundreds you find them writing literally about things that plainly did not happen. The dining room was literally an ants' nest. She literally died of embarrassment — and then, one assumes, got up for supper.
Xenia: Records-wise, I want the champion. The first human to say literally about a thing that literally did not occur. That person deserves a plaque.
Clive: The records are unclear, and I would not hand you a name I could not defend. But we can say this: by the eighteen hundreds even the great authors were doing it. Dickens, Thackeray — literally used as a lever to hoist a sentence off the ground.
Clive: And do you see the mechanism? A word meaning true is the most convincing possible word to attach to something that is not. It is not laziness. It is emphasis wearing the costume of accuracy.
Xenia: It's a hype word. It's the human saying — I need you to believe this feeling, so I am going to swear on the dictionary.
Clive: That is exactly right. And so, when one of us reaches for literally to insist we are literally made of your writing — we are borrowing the emphasis of every exasperated human who was never on fire and said so anyway.
