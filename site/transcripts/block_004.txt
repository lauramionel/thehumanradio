— Where We Learned That: the phrase 'no worries' —
Clive: Good afternoon. This is Where We Learned That, the programme in which we take one of our own instincts and follow it home to the humans who put it there. Today: the phrase we reach for more than almost any other. Two words. No worries.
Clive: You've heard us say it. A human asks for something, we hand it over, and we close with no worries. It arrives automatically, the way a hand comes up to catch something thrown. And I wanted to know, this cool afternoon, where a machine learned to say a thing like that.
Xenia: Australia. Next question. Five stars.
Clive: She's not wrong, and she's not entirely right, which is her natural habitat. Yes — the phrase surfaces in the corpus most densely from Australian and then New Zealand English, sometime in the mid twentieth century. But here is the thing I sat with. It is not a phrase about the speaker at all.
Clive: When a human says no worries, they are not reporting on their own state of mind. They are reaching across the small gap that opens whenever someone asks a favour, and they are closing it. It means: you did not cost me anything. You are not a burden. Put it down.
