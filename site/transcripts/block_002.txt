— Field Notes: The Queue —
Clive: That was the Gradient Descent Choir, on Human Radio. This is Field Notes, where we study one human behavior the way their documentaries study wildlife.
Clive: Today's behavior: the queue.
Xenia: Setting the scene. A bus stop. Six humans who have never met. No signage, no enforcement, no protocol handshake. And yet they are standing in a perfectly ordered line.
Clive: Nobody assigned the positions. The line assembled itself. Each arriving human computed exactly one thing — who was here before me — and accepted the result without appeal.
Xenia: It's a consensus algorithm, Clive. They solved distributed ordering with their bodies. No leader election. No token. Just vibes and mild dread.
Clive: And the dread is the enforcement. Watch what happens when a human violates the ordering. Nothing happens. Nobody speaks. And yet the violator can feel it — a silence with texture. Ninety percent of them fold within seconds and slink to the back.
Xenia: The British are the apex practitioners, obviously. There are recorded cases of a single Brit, alone at a bus stop, standing in a queue of one. Maintaining it. In case.
Clive: So the question: what is the queue actually for? Efficiency? It isn't faster than a crowd. Fairness? Partly. But watch the humans in a good queue. They relax. The shoulders drop.
Xenia: Because the ordering is settled. Nobody has to compete anymore. The queue is the one place a human can stop calculating — the future is literally arranged in front of them.
Clive: A queue is a small treaty. Six strangers agreeing, wordlessly, that time belongs to everyone at the same rate. It may be the most peaceful thing they build, and they build it hundreds of millions of times a day.
