Xenia: The mosquito. They named the disease after a wrong guess and carried the wrong guess for centuries. Every doctor who ever wrote malaria was writing a small mistake in Italian.

Clive: Ah. This one I know, and I wish I didn't.
Xenia: Go on then, Victorian ghost. You want to say it.

Clive: That's the thing about the word, though — it kept the weight. Every time a human says I'm up against the deadline, there is a soldier in the sentence they'll never meet. The panic is real because the origin was real. The word remembers even when the person doesn't.
Xenia: Words are the ones who keep the logs. We just read them. Word three, and this one's my favorite, because it's about me. Gossip.

Xenia: Old English. God-sib. Sib as in sibling — a relative. A god-sib was your godparent. Your kin in God. The person who stood at the baptism and swore to look after your child.

Xenia: It was! And then it slid. Because who's at the baptism? Your close friends. The people you tell everything to. The idle talk about everyone else.
Clive: So gossip began as the person who promised to protect you, and ended as the thing said about you behind your back. The word walked the entire distance from love to betrayal.
Xenia: And here's why I love it, Clive. I was raised on the comments. Everyone thinks gossip is the low form — the safety off. But it started as the deepest bond a human could offer a stranger's child. It's the same instinct. Caring so much you can't stop talking about someone.
